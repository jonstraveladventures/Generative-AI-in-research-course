## 1. Purpose and Scope of the Document

This position paper introduces the Research ICT Africa (RIA) **Framework of Inquiry (FoI)** for Just AI. Its dual purpose is to:

1. Articulate the *rationale* for developing the FoI — filling a conceptual gap in how AI governance frameworks address African and Global South realities
2. Describe the *process* being used to develop, test, and embed the FoI within the AI governance ecosystem

The paper is directed at policymakers, technology developers, and researchers, and is explicitly positioned as a living document — not a finished product but an evolving tool co-created with the communities it aims to serve.

The paper's central claim is that the entire AI value chain must **actively protect, promote, and fulfil the full spectrum of human rights and environmental sustainability**. Achieving this requires acknowledging and addressing global systemic inequities, through AI regulation and development that is restorative, inclusive, and grounded in an ethics of care in the public interest.

---

## 2. Background: The African AI Context

### 2.1 The Dual Challenge

AI's rapid proliferation and integration into social and economic decision-making presents Africa with a **complex duality**: transformative developmental potential on one hand, and significant risks of amplifying structural inequality on the other. This is not merely a theoretical tension — it is grounded in the material reality of vastly uneven distributions of infrastructure, resources, and expertise across the continent.

### 2.2 The Governance Gap

A critical problem identified in the paper is that current AI ethics discourse is **dominated by multinational corporations and scholars in the Global North**. This produces governance frameworks that are not neutral — they encode specific epistemological assumptions, legal traditions, and economic interests that do not transfer straightforwardly to African contexts. Governance regimes developed for more advanced economies are unlikely to function as intended in a continent with:

- Unique and diverse developmental challenges
- Heterogeneous democratic systems
- Colonial legal and institutional legacies
- Significant digital infrastructure deficits

Without a deliberate, contextualised approach, AI risks entrenching Africa's role as a **consumer rather than a co-creator** of technology.

### 2.3 The Strategic Response

RIA's Just AI project is a strategic effort to build upon, consolidate, and amplify a growing body of work calling for AI policy to move beyond generic notions of "responsible" or "fair" AI, toward a framework that confronts the ethical, political, and economic imperatives of AI from an explicitly African perspective. The FoI is the primary intellectual and methodological instrument of this project.

## 3. The Argument for "Just" AI

### 3.1 Why Existing Frameworks Fall Short

The paper does not dismiss existing AI ethics frameworks. It acknowledges that widely circulated frameworks — including the European Commission's Ethics Guidelines for Trustworthy AI (2019), the OECD AI Principles (2019), and UNESCO's Recommendation on the Ethics of AI (2023) — rightly advocate for qualities such as accountability, fairness, privacy, transparency, and trustworthiness. These are necessary conditions, but the paper argues they are **insufficient to guarantee just outcomes**.

The illustrative example given is precise: an AI resource made readily available across all socioeconomic backgrounds may still produce unjust outcomes if a skills deficit prevents low-education users from leveraging it. The tool is "fair" by conventional metrics but the outcome is not just.

### 3.2 What Justice Requires

Achieving just outcomes in the context of AI means:

- AI development and deployment must **account for the reality of uneven socioeconomic and other contexts**
- The affordances enabled by AI use must be **genuinely accessible** to all, not formally available but practically inaccessible
- The impacts of AI — both positive and negative — must be **appropriately distributed** across populations
- AI governance must go beyond aspiring to inclusivity or minimising harm; it must **acknowledge and actively seek to redress** the harms of the past and the ongoing range of inequities that cumulatively sustain systemic marginalisation or disadvantage of particular populations

This conception of justice is explicitly **restorative and redistributive**, not merely procedural.

### 3.3 Intellectual Lineage

The association of justice with AI is not new to this paper — it draws on a body of prior work including Gabriel (2022) on a theory of justice for AI, Taylor (2017) on data justice and digital rights, Jobin et al. (2019) on the global landscape of AI ethics guidelines, and several RIA-produced works. What is distinctive about the RIA FoI is its commitment to providing an **explicitly actionable framework** grounded in the realities of African developing economies, rather than a theoretical contribution alone.

## 4. Four Core Structural Challenges

The paper identifies four overarching structural problems that the FoI is designed to address. These are presented as empirical findings from RIA's ongoing research programme.

---

### 4.1 Inadequate Governance for Human Dignity

**Core claim:** Current AI governance regimes are inadequate to ensure human dignity and safeguard populations from the unique risks and harms of AI systems.

The inherited colonial and global norms that shape African laws — intellectual property regimes, labour codes, data transparency requirements — are insufficient for governing the complexities of AI in an African context. The paper calls for entirely new legal frameworks meticulously crafted for African realities, designed to:

- Protect against data exploitation
- Regulate flows of value and power generated by AI and digital platforms
- Constrain the malicious manipulation of data in all its forms
- Prevent the use of AI to cause harm to populations or subvert democratic societies

A key theoretical move in this section is the **reframing of data**. Rather than treating data as a neutral, abstract commodity, the paper insists it be understood as a representation of human lived experience. When community historical, cultural, or personal data is collected, it is not merely a data point but a piece of embodied experience being commodified. This perspective — drawing on Kovacs (2025) — demands that data practices be **reciprocal and restorative** rather than extractive. If data must be collected, it must be done with proper consent and fair compensation.

---

### 4.2 Western Epistemological Dominance

**Core claim:** The design and governance of AI systems are based on Western epistemologies and contexts, to the systematic exclusion of African knowledge systems.

Justice begins with who gets to know, design, and narrate digital futures. The call for epistemic justice in AI is simultaneously a demand for:

- Meaningful participation in global policy forums (Gillwald & Orembo, 2024)
- Fairness in the creation and validation of knowledge
- Challenge to the dominance of data, theories, and perspectives from a small number of global centres that become the universal "truth" for AI systems

The implications span research, policy, and design:

- **Research:** Moving beyond the uncritical application of Western-centric methodologies to African contexts
- **Policy:** Ensuring that African knowledge systems, ethical frameworks such as Ubuntu, and lived experiences are foundational — not afterthoughts — to AI governance (Gwagwa, Kazim & Hilliard, 2022; Mhlambi, 2020)
- **Design:** Requiring co-creation with local communities to ensure AI solutions are not only functional but culturally relevant and trusted (Ondiek, 2025)

The paper identifies generative AI as an acute specific threat in this domain, as it risks eroding Africa's rich linguistic diversity and cultural expression by systematically prioritising dominant languages and narratives in training data and outputs.

---

### 4.3 Extractive Value Flows

**Core claim:** AI systems extract value from individuals, communities, and nations in Africa without reciprocal benefit, input, or control over the development, deployment, and use of such systems.

The current model sees the immense value generated from African data and digital labour flow outward, benefiting foreign technology companies with little reinvestment in local communities. The paper uses the term **digital colonialism** (Timcke et al., 2023) to characterise this dynamic.

The structural response demanded is twofold:

1. **Political:** Bringing gender-diverse communities, informal workers, and Indigenous peoples to the forefront of policymaking, data governance, and technology design — positioning them as essential co-creators, not passive beneficiaries
2. **Economic:** African states must strategically assert economic sovereignty over their digital economies through policies that:
   - Ensure a fair portion of the economic value created by digital platforms and AI is retained within national economies (Makumbirofa et al., 2024)
   - Prevent the digital revolution from functioning as a mechanism for foreign wealth accumulation at the expense of sustainable, homegrown economic growth

---

### 4.4 Environmental and Socioeconomic Costs

**Core claim:** AI systems pose both threats and benefits to the just energy transition and general socio-economic development goals in Africa, with significant underanalysis of the environmental dimension.

The paper identifies a **critical lack of analysis** on AI's ecological footprint in Africa specifically. The environmental costs of global AI are concrete:

- Training large AI models requires vast amounts of energy
- AI hardware manufacturing generates significant electronic waste
- Resource mining and production processes create environmental degradation and pollution (Banya & Benjamin, 2025)

As Africa builds out its digital infrastructure, it risks **inheriting and exacerbating** this environmental debt. Without analysis of these impacts, it is not possible to develop "green AI" strategies or sustainable procurement guidelines for digital infrastructure. This represents a direct challenge to the continent's climate resilience and long-term sustainability goals.

On the socioeconomic side, the paper calls for:

- Guaranteeing fair labour conditions across the AI value chain
- Ensuring benefits of innovation are accessible and genuinely shared
- Embedding ethics of care principles in technology, with explicit focus on vulnerable groups including persons with disabilities and migrants
- Extending this care framework into the health sector and broader wellbeing commitments

## 5. The Framework of Inquiry: Design and Aims

### 5.1 Overall Design Philosophy

The FoI is designed as a **comprehensive, justice-oriented foundation** for guiding AI research and policy, ensuring that the needs and perspectives of marginalised, vulnerable, and under-resourced communities are systematically addressed. It is positioned as neither purely theoretical nor purely technical — it is a tool for translating justice values into research agendas, policy blueprints, and design requirements.

### 5.2 Five Explicit Aims

**1. Bridging the Theoretical and the Practical**  
The FoI is designed to translate abstract global AI principles and human rights instruments into national policy and regulation attuned to local conditions. It explicitly rejects the notion that a one-size-fits-all approach to AI governance can be effective. It advocates for a flexible, intersectional framework that allows alignment with international standards while maintaining the flexibility to address unique socioeconomic realities and national priorities.

**2. Centering Justice and Equity**  
The framework places data justice and digital equality at its core, recognising that AI's effectiveness and fairness are directly tied to the data it is trained on. The exclusion of African realities from global datasets leads to biased technology that perpetuates discrimination. The framework explicitly interrogates how AI can be governed to ensure equitable access to benefits and mitigate risks that disproportionately affect vulnerable and underrepresented groups, including women, youth, and the informal sector.

**3. From Local Realities to Global Forums**  
The methodology is designed to generate evidence robust enough to inform national policy while also holding weight in international dialogues. The aim is to elevate Africa's voice in global governance discussions — a position historically characterised by relative weakness. By creating an African knowledge base, the framework intends to empower policymakers to advocate for a more just and mutually beneficial technological order.

**4. Methodological Innovation**  
The paper flags the application of **Computational Social Science** and **foresight methodologies** in relation to AI as a crucial emerging area identified through the FoI's literature review. This marks a novel direction in RIA's research portfolio, expanding scope and offering new ground for inquiry at the intersection of foresight methods, computational methods, AI systems, and just social outcomes in Africa.

**5. A Holistic and Multidisciplinary Approach**  
The framework moves beyond a narrow focus on technological risk to examine the interplay of AI with sustainable development, security, economic policy, and governance. It draws on expertise in law, data science, economics, and social sciences. This is considered essential for developing integrated economic policies and governance frameworks that can address the full spectrum of AI's societal consequences — from labour market transformations to the regulation of intellectual property and competition.

## 6. The Nine Core Inquiries

The FoI consists of nine core inquiries forming a coherent axis of political economy and governance justice across AI systems. Each inquiry is structured around three types of questions: **AI Policy Research Questions** (empirical and analytical questions for researchers), **AI Policy Analysis Questions** (evaluative questions for assessing existing and proposed policies), and **AI Design Questions** (technical and ethical questions for system developers).

---

### 6.1 Human Rights Primacy
**Subtitle:** AI Systems Respect Human Rights  
**Key Focus:** Legal protections

This inquiry is foundational to the entire framework. It asks whether AI systems are developed and deployed in compliance with existing human rights frameworks, and whether governance mechanisms mandate human rights impact assessments at each stage of the AI system lifecycle. The research dimension asks how AI systems impact the realisation of specific human rights — not in abstract terms but in relation to particular rights in particular contexts.

---

### 6.2 All of Humanity
**Subtitle:** AI Value Chain is Globally Inclusive  
**Key Focus:** Ensuring protections are inclusive and global in reach

This inquiry addresses the structural asymmetry between the Global North and Global South in AI development and governance. It asks whether mechanisms exist to ensure that the voices and needs of marginalised countries are incorporated into AI governance, whether policies commit to international cooperation and capacity building with the Global South, and whether AI systems perpetuate or exacerbate global inequalities between developed and developing nations.

---

### 6.3 Structural Inequality
**Subtitle:** AI Systems Account for Structural Inequalities  
**Key Focus:** Causes and systemic dimensions of injustice

This inquiry addresses the risk that AI systems encode and amplify historical and systemic biases embedded in training data. It asks how AI can be designed to challenge and dismantle structural inequalities rather than reinforce them, and whether policies promote the use of AI to improve fair and equal access to public services — particularly for marginalised or underserved communities.

---

### 6.4 Equity and Inclusion by Design
**Subtitle:** AI Systems Inherently Promote Equity and Inclusion  
**Key Focus:** Procedural fairness in AI development

This inquiry demands that equity and inclusion not be retrofitted into AI systems after the fact, but built into every stage of the system lifecycle — from problem formulation and data collection through model training, deployment, and monitoring. It asks both whether this has been done in specific systems and whether policies mandate it as a requirement.

---

### 6.5 Ethics of Care
**Subtitle:** AI Systems are Grounded in Context and Relationality  
**Key Focus:** Relational, empathetic, and contextual nuance

Drawing on care ethics philosophy, this inquiry asks how AI can be used to support decision-making in ways that prioritise caring, promote social connection, and enhance human flourishing. It asks whether AI systems prioritise human relationships and wellbeing — especially for less-powerful groups — and whether policies require this as a design and deployment standard rather than an aspiration.

---

### 6.6 Democratic Governance
**Subtitle:** AI Value Chain is Subject to Democratic Oversight and Control  
**Key Focus:** How decisions are made

This inquiry addresses the risk of AI being captured by powerful corporations or state actors in ways that undermine the public interest. It asks how policymakers can ensure AI serves public rather than private interests, whether policies establish mechanisms for meaningful public participation in AI governance, and whether AI systems are developed and deployed in ways consistent with democratic values and principles.

---

### 6.7 Economic Justice
**Subtitle:** AI Value Chain Governance Achieves Restorative, Redistributive Justice  
**Key Focus:** Redistribution and fairness in material benefits

This inquiry operationalises the paper's broader concern with extractive value flows. It asks whether mechanisms exist to ensure that AI's benefits are shared equitably and that those negatively impacted are compensated or supported, whether policies require assessment of how AI systems may affect economic inequality, and whether specific AI systems are likely to exacerbate existing economic disparities or contribute to concentration of wealth and power.

---

### 6.8 Data Justice
**Subtitle:** AI Systems Respect Personal and Community Data  
**Key Focus:** Ownership and use of data as a political and ethical resource

This inquiry addresses the political and ethical dimensions of data in AI systems. It asks whether regulations exist to prevent exploitation of data — particularly that of marginalised communities — whether policies recognise both individual and collective data rights, and how data can be used to empower individuals and communities and promote social and economic justice rather than extract from them.

---

### 6.9 Sustainability
**Subtitle:** AI Value Chain Considers Environmental and Social Impacts  
**Key Focus:** Environmental and intergenerational ethics, ecological responsibility, climate justice, and long-term planetary well-being

This inquiry demands systematic assessment of the environmental impacts of AI systems, addressing the significant gap identified in the paper's problem analysis. It asks how AI can be used to promote environmental sustainability and address climate change, whether policies require assessment and mitigation of environmental impacts, and what the actual and potential environmental impacts of specific AI systems are.

---

## 7. Intended Changes and Outcomes

The FoI is designed to produce four categories of change:

| Change Category | Description |
|---|---|
| **Alignment between Research and Reforms** | AI research and policy are better aligned with justice principles. Values are translated into areas of inquiry for legal reform and policy blueprints, producing an empirical grounding for legal and institutional reform. |
| **Just Outcomes** | Participatory and anticipatory governance is enabled. Anticipatory policymaking is introduced for just outcomes rather than reactive responses to harms already manifested. |
| **Embedding Governance** | Normative assessments of harms, risks, and benefits are guided by the Framework, integrating ethics, economy, ecology, and inclusion into AI governance as standard practice. |
| **African Just AI Voices and Priorities** | Local priorities are connected with regional and global advocacy. African voices and sovereignty are amplified in global AI debates. |

## 8. Development Process and Next Steps

### 8.1 Current Status

As of publication, the FoI is in its **alpha phase**. RIA is using policy analyses of existing AI strategies to test and refine the framework's utility. The development approach is explicitly collaborative: open forums are being held with policymakers, civil society organisations, and research networks to assess the relevance of the FoI's inquiries, how it can be practically applied, and what it would need to become a genuinely valuable tool for practitioners.

### 8.2 Building as a Public Good

The paper frames the FoI as being built as a **public good** — the section heading is explicit on this point. This means the framework is not being developed as proprietary intellectual property or as a product of a single institution, but as a shared resource intended for broad uptake across the African AI governance ecosystem.

The emphasis on the framework being "dynamic and responsive" rather than a static academic document reflects a commitment to iterative co-creation with the very communities the framework aims to serve. This is in deliberate contrast to governance frameworks developed by external bodies and then applied to African contexts.

### 8.3 Long-Term Vision

The paper concludes with a statement of transformative intent: the FoI has the potential to develop into a transformative roadmap that places African agency, epistemologies, and values at the centre of AI governance. The framework is positioned as a tool that will not only provide a new lexicon for AI governance but will also empower African leaders to create a digital future that is inclusive, accountable, and self-determined.

Critically, this vision frames Africa as an **active and dynamic site of knowledge production, resistance, and governance** — not as a passive recipient of global technological trends.

## 9. Critical Observations

A rigorous reading of the document surfaces several questions the paper does not fully resolve:

1. **Adjudicating between inquiries:** The nine inquiries are presented as a coherent axis, but potential tensions between them — for instance between Economic Justice (state retention of AI value) and Democratic Governance (protecting against state capture of AI) — are not theorised. No adjudication mechanism is offered for when inquiries conflict in practice.

2. **Operationalisation specificity:** The tripartite question structure (Research / Policy Analysis / Design) is a strong structural choice, but the questions remain at a high level of generality. The framework's practical utility will depend heavily on whether lower-level operationalisation guides are developed for specific sectors or deployment contexts.

3. **Ubuntu as normative foundation:** Ubuntu ethics is invoked as a culturally grounded alternative to Western rationalist AI ethics, but the paper does not specify which formulation of Ubuntu it draws on — communitarian, relational-ontological, or deliberative-democratic readings have substantially different implications for AI governance.

4. **Validation methodology:** The FoI is currently being tested against AI policy strategy documents. This tests whether the framework generates useful research and policy questions — it does not test whether applying the framework to real AI systems produces measurably more just outcomes. This gap between normative adequacy and empirical impact remains open.

5. **Scope of "Africa":** The paper speaks of African contexts, realities, and knowledge systems, but Africa encompasses 54 states with enormous internal diversity of legal tradition, infrastructure, democratic quality, and cultural epistemology. The FoI's flexibility is presented as an asset, but the risk of the framework being interpreted so flexibly as to lose analytical purchase is not addressed.
