## 5.4 — The Hallucinated Citation Crisis

| File | Citation |
|------|----------|
| `Magesh_2024_Hallucination-Free-Legal-AI.pdf` | Magesh, V., Surani, F., Dahl, M., Suzgun, M., Manning, C. D., & Ho, D. E. (2024). Hallucination-Free? Assessing the Reliability of Leading AI Legal Research Tools. [arXiv:2405.20362](https://arxiv.org/abs/2405.20362) — Stanford HAI study, 17–34 % hallucinations on purpose-built legal tools, 58–82 % on general chatbots. |
| `Chelli_2024_Hallucinations-Systematic-Reviews.pdf` | Chelli, M., Descamps, J., Lavoué, V., et al. (2024). Hallucination Rates and Reference Accuracy of ChatGPT and Bard for Systematic Reviews. *JMIR* 26: e53164. [DOI:10.2196/53164](https://doi.org/10.2196/53164) — GPT-3.5 39.6 %, GPT-4 28.6 %, Bard 91.4 %. |
| `Linardon_2025_Mental-Health-Hallucinations.pdf` | Linardon, J., et al. (2025). Hallucinations in AI-Generated References for Mental Health Literature Reviews. *JMIR Mental Health* 12: e80371. [DOI:10.2196/80371](https://doi.org/10.2196/80371) — GPT-4o 19.9 % overall, 28–29 % for niche topics, 6 % for well-studied. |
| `Niimi_2025_Citation-Frequency-Hallucination.pdf` | Niimi, J. (2025). Hallucinations in Bibliographic Recommendation: Citation Frequency as a Proxy for Training Data Redundancy. [arXiv:2510.25378](https://arxiv.org/abs/2510.25378) |
